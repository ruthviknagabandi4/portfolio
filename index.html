AI Lab

A working AI chatbot built on Chatbase.co that guides users through structured ethical reflection on AI scenarios using a Socratic questioning approach — demonstrating practical AI development skills, human-centered design thinking, and the ability to build tools that serve genuine educational and professional needs.

Objective

To gain direct hands-on experience designing, building, and iterating on a functional AI assistant, applying a Design Thinking framework to produce a purpose-built tool that serves a genuine user need in AI ethics education.

Description

The Ethics in AI Reflection Guide is a chatbot that does not give answers — it asks questions. Using a six-step Socratic framework, it guides users through identifying stakeholders, surfacing ethical frameworks, applying one framework, encountering a complication, and forming their own reasoned conclusion.

2

3

Ideate → Prototype → Test: Chose Socratic approach over prescriptive bot. Built on Chatbase.co. Iterated three times to balance depth with usability.

Artifact 02 — Workshop 2 — Assignment 2.3

ML vs. Deep Learning — An Operations Analyst's Perspective

Written Analysis

A comparative analysis of machine learning and deep learning through two real-world case studies — Zillow's Zestimate (Linear Regression) and Tesla's Autopilot (CNN + Deep Reinforcement Learning). Written from the perspective of an operations professional who thinks about reliability, interpretability, and production readiness — not just model accuracy.

Objective

To demonstrate the ability to differentiate between machine learning and deep learning by analyzing real-world applications — and to connect that analytical capability to the operational question that matters most: which approach is reliable, interpretable, and production-ready for the problem at hand?

Description

The analysis examines Zillow's use of regression-based ML for home price prediction — where structured tabular data and user trust requirements make traditional ML ideal — and Tesla Autopilot's use of CNNs and deep reinforcement learning — where raw sensor data processing under real-world variability makes deep learning the only viable approach.

Case Study 1 — ML: Zillow Zestimate (Linear Regression)

✓ Why ML is Suitable

Structured tabular data with clearly interpretable features — square footage, bedrooms, neighborhood quality. Regression models are fast, explainable, and trusted by consumers. Zillow can tell a homeowner exactly why their estimate changed. Tabular real estate data does not require the scale that DL demands to outperform simpler approaches (Couto, 2025).

✗ Why Deep Learning is NOT Suitable

House price data is structured and tabular — not the high-dimensional, unstructured data DL is designed for. A deep neural network would be harder to interpret, more expensive to train, and unlikely to outperform a well-tuned gradient boosting model. The interpretability loss alone would make it unsuitable for a consumer-facing product.

Case Study 2 — Deep Learning: Tesla Autopilot (CNN + Deep RL)

✓ Why DL is Suitable

Autonomous driving requires processing raw, unstructured sensory input — camera images, LIDAR, radar — in real time under enormous variability in lighting, weather, and road conditions. CNNs automatically learn hierarchical visual representations layer by layer. No human could manually engineer features to recognize a partially obscured pedestrian at 70mph (Sanderson, 2023).

✗ Why Traditional ML is NOT Suitable

Manual feature engineering cannot capture the complexity and variability of real-world visual environments. A decision tree or SVM fed hand-crafted features would be brittle, slow, and dangerously inaccurate in the unpredictable conditions autonomous vehicles must navigate safely. The stakes are too high for brittle models.

Concepts Demonstrated

Linear Regression Supervised Learning Convolutional Neural Networks Deep Reinforcement Learning Model Interpretability Real-World Application Analysis

Value Proposition

For operations leaders and technology managers, this artifact demonstrates the structured analytical thinking that operations professionals bring to AI/ML decisions. The choice between ML and DL is not about technical sophistication — it is about reliability, interpretability, and fitness for purpose. Those are the same values that drive effective batch operations work, and they translate directly to better AI/ML implementation decisions.

Unique Value

My operations background shaped this analysis around production readiness — a lens most ML analyses miss. Zillow's Zestimate needs to be explainable to millions of consumers. Tesla's Autopilot needs to be reliable enough that lives depend on it. Those operational requirements — not just accuracy metrics — are what determine which approach is right. Operations professionals think about that by default.

Relevance

As AI systems are integrated into enterprise operations, operations professionals will be on the front lines of keeping them running reliably. Understanding which AI architectures are interpretable, which are black boxes, which require massive compute infrastructure, and which can run efficiently on existing hardware — these are operational questions as much as technical ones. This artifact demonstrates that understanding applied to real production systems.

Artifact 03 — Workshop 3 — Discussion 3.4

Neural Networks — What If We Started With the Human, Not the Machine?

Communication for Learning

A reflective, human-centered explanation of neural networks developed in collaboration with Google Gemini — using the universal experience of learning to read as the central analogy. Demonstrates the ability to make complex AI/ML concepts accessible through storytelling, progressive disclosure, and multi-perspective audience testing. Written for operations professionals and technology leaders who need to understand AI systems without losing the human dimensions that make those systems matter.

Objective

To apply communication for learning strategies to explain neural networks through an analogy rooted in universal human experience — demonstrating that the most effective AI explanations begin with what the audience already knows, not with what the explainer wants to teach.

Description

The explanation uses the childhood experience of learning to read as the central analogy — connecting neural network training directly to a learning process every person has been through. The explanation ends with a deliberate philosophical note inviting the reader to see the parallel between human and machine learning as meaningful rather than merely technical.

The Explanation — Starting With the Human

🌱 Think about how you learned to read.

Nobody handed you a rulebook of grammar and said "memorize this and you will be able to read." You saw words. Thousands and thousands of words. Over years, you started to recognize patterns — this combination of letters makes this sound, this shape means this word, this sentence structure signals this meaning.

You were never explicitly taught most of what you know about language. You absorbed it through exposure and feedback. You got things wrong, someone corrected you, and gradually the corrections became unnecessary because you had internalized the patterns.

That is exactly how a neural network learns.

It does not receive instructions. It receives examples — thousands, sometimes millions of them. Each time it makes a prediction, it compares that prediction to the correct answer. If it is wrong, it adjusts — slightly, precisely, repeatedly. Over enough iterations, those adjustments accumulate into something that looks remarkably like understanding.

Learning, it turns out, works the same way whether the learner is human or machine.

Communication Strategies Applied

Relevance & Context

Learning to read is a universal human experience — every audience member has lived it, making the analogy immediately accessible.

Storytelling

Built a narrative arc — the child who cannot read, the long process of exposure and correction, the eventual fluency — mirroring exactly what a neural network experiences.

Feedback & Reflection

The closing philosophical note invites the reader to reflect on their own experience as a window into the technology — making understanding personal rather than abstract.

Multi-Perspective Testing

Asked Gemini to simulate three reader perspectives — teacher, nurse, and first-generation student — discovering that no single analogy reaches everyone equally.

AI-Assisted Process

1

Perspective Simulation: Asked Google Gemini to simulate three different reader perspectives before committing to one analogy — teacher, nurse, and first-generation student each responded differently.

2

Analogy Selection: The reading analogy resonated most broadly across all three simulated perspectives — it precedes any professional identity and connects to a shared human developmental experience.

3

Philosophical Framing: Gemini suggested the closing parallel — "learning works the same way whether the learner is human or machine" — as a way to invite reflection rather than simply deliver information.

4

Refinement: Removed every technical term that did not survive the nurse and first-generation student reading tests. Final version is accessible without being condescending.

Operations leaders who cannot explain how these systems learn cannot effectively evaluate whether they are working correctly, advocate for their improvement, or protect their teams from over-relying on outputs that may not generalize to new conditions. This artifact demonstrates the communication foundation that responsible AI operations leadership requires.
